Smart Farming Regulations

An argument for the governemnt to create legsliation to govern smart farming

Posted by Janavi Chadha on April 27, 2021

As discussed in the previous posts Smart Farming has a plethora of benefits but it also does have various downsides and raises a number of questions. The first question I would like to discuss is the question of who owns the data that is created through Smart farming. This is a common issue in almost every industry involving the production of data and smart farming is no exception, does the farmer who is utilizing the tools have access to the tools or own it? Does the company that created the tool own the data? What are the regulations surrounding this data?

When we deal with data corresponding with users there are very strict bounds on what can and what cannot be done. This makes sense especially when considering that a lot of user data has important aspects that can potentially put a person in danger if not properly protected. Regulations like these have no been put into place surrounding Smart Farming technology. The fact that legislation has not yet been created makes sense since the immediate concerns that arise with user data do not apply to the same extent when dealing with agricultural data but that does not make this question any less important and until we find a solution we will not be able to widely use such equipment.

We need the government to create a regulatory system that does not give too much power to a single stakeholder while also ensuring that those who get access to the data properly use it. By providing this data to a larger group we also open ourselves for the potential misuse of data which itself will come with a large number of challenges. I propose that we create a system where the company that creates the tool owns the data but is still required to share it with the farmer. This would be invoking utilitarianism ethics since we are following this action for the benefit of the masses at the expense of the single company that must share their collected data. Despite the fact that some will experience the “reverse of happiness” this would still be the right action to take as they promote happiness in the majority by promoting equality between the stakeholders. By sharing collected data we will create transparency within this process which will make it easier to deal with cases of errors or mismanagement.

In the space of Autonomous vehicles, a hotly contested topic is the question of who is at fault in the case of an accident, a similar question also exists in the arena of Smart Farming. To draw from an example from PNAS: If fungicide has been applied to plants too late and there are traces remaining when the farmer goes to harvest who is at fault? Is it the farmer, the software creator, or the creator of the sensor? We can see how questions that deal with the intersection between machine learning, technology, and humans don’t have straightforward answers which means that it will only be more and more difficult to create effective legislation.

Just as it is in the case of autonomous vehicles, time is of the essence in this case. The more time spend delaying the creation of legislation to govern Smart Farming the higher chance that we will not be able to create effective legislation. I recommend that in conjunction with the grants suggested in the last blog posts a board is created within the government consisting of both government officials as well as industry experts to discuss legislation. I believe that this proposed board consisting of both engineers as well as government officials would be able to effectively navigate the intricacies of this field and the intersection between these fields.
