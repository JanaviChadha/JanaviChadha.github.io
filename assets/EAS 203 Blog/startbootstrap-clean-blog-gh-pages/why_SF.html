Why Smart Farming?

An argument for the widespread use of smart farming techniques

Posted by Janavi Chadha on April 13, 2021

Smart Farming is quite a popular buzzword right now but what is it and why should we use it?

Smart Farming is the process of using the internet of things and various other smart technologies to make farming processes as efficient as possible. Allowing for the maximum utilization of resources while reducing the need for human labor1. Examples of the technologies utilized within Smart Farming are water sensors, photosensors, temperature sensors among others2. These are utilized to collect data to then determine what nutrients and resources a plant needs to survive and produce it's resources ( fruit, grain, etc. )

But why use Smart Farming over the current agricultural system that we have in place? To put it simply our current agricultural system is wasteful. On average farms around the world account for 70% of water usage and of that 40% is lost in the environment due to poor irrigation systems, evaporation ,and overall poor water management3. With smart farming a plant is only provided with the nutrients it needs to survive to produce the product we need. This means that extra materials such as pesticides or fertilizers are not given in excess and wanted and the materials such as water can instead be re-routed for other uses such as human consumption or to grow more plants. Smart farming as well as reducing the use of materials reduces the need for human labor automating many processes such as spacing and planting. When the work cannot be automated everything is done at peak times in optimal conditions so no work is wasted. Combining both of these factors to our benefit means that we have a great potential to reduce the harm that our unsustainable farming practices have upon our planet. At this point agriculture accounts for around 10% of annual greenhouse gas emissions according to the EPA 4. By optimizing all processes related to farming we will be able to reduce this percentage and help alleviate the pressure that agriculture puts on the global warming crisis.

Despite these above benefits, incorporating Smart Farming into our current agricultural system is much easier said than done. When considering the current agricultural production chain there are many moving components and adding smart farming will only make it more complicated. Within the simplified agricultural chain now we have the producers who are the farmers, the government who regulates the producers and the consumers. For the sake of simplicity, I will leave out the middlemen who buy the produce from the farmers and then sell to stores that then sell to the consumers. When we bring smart farming into the chain though we add on the companies who produce the technologies and will monitor the produce and implement the necessary measures to ensure that the crops are growing healthy with the use of the least resources. These are all of the stakeholders within our Smart Agricultural chain and we need to ensure that each stakeholder gets their needs met but also that one stakeholder does not have too much power over a certain aspect of this process. In the following blog posts, I will outline my recommendations for the actions we should take to ensure that this does not occur. This includes grants from the government encouraging research into Smart Farming as well as updated and clearer legislation surrounding data and usages.
