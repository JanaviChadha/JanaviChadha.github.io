Is Smart Farming Accessible?

An argument for the use of government grants to fund Smart Farming Research

Posted by Janavi Chadha on April 20, 2021

Agriculture is a field that is often revolutionized by the invention of new technologies. We saw this occur in the 1950s - 1960s with the widespread use of tools such as pesticides as well as tractors in farming during the green revolution. While those tools come with their own challenges they are now an integral part of the farming system and we would lose a significant part of the system if we were to lose these technologies and inventions. The Smart Farming Revolution is shaping up to be as big as these past revolutions if not bigger. Smart Farming allows farmers to reduce the usage of materials as well as reduce the need for human labor. Smart farming builds upon the tools from previous generations too, for example, there is a robot that utilizes use vision software to detect plants, and use highly pressurized canisters of positives to deliver pesticides directly to the plant eliminating the need for systems where helicopters or airplanes deposit pesticides over field leading to mass runoff of the excess and eventually leading the ecosystem destruction with Algae Blooms and other such problems. Below is an image of this machine, the See and Spray, along with an image explaining how it functions.1

Image of the See and Spray a machine the utilizes machine learning to detetect weeds and deliver accurate jets of pesticides into the ground Slide covering how the See and Spray functions

To achieve all of these benefits that come with smart farming requires the use of many machines and complicated algorithms that all come at a high cost to the farmer / business and even once the machines have been created there is a need for constant internet connection so that data can be stored and the machine can function properly. This high initial cost can make the technology seem out of reach for most small farmers. To add to the cost there are a limited number of manufactures of such products which means that as of now the few companies that do exist hold a monopoly on the market. Despite this high cost now there is hope for the future, since this technology deals with data, manufacturers recognize the benefit that comes from more consumers ( more data and more accurate predictions ). This means that if we were to get more machines out into the market we would not only be able to create a more accurate end product which will raise the popularity and hopefully in turn create more manufacturers.

I would like to propose that to encourage the use of Smart Farming technologies the governments provide subsidies to farmers to afford these technologies. And I would also like to propose that the government make research grants available for studying and developing technologies related to this field. Having the ability to produce more food and a lower cost is a prospect that would be quite beneficial for the economy and we know from our modules that when the government is able to support research into a specific field or idea then that field is able to advance farther in that time period than they would without the support of the government.

This legislation that would allow such grants to be possible would be similar to the Morril Acts of 1862 and 1890 that funded educational incisions by providing them land that they could then sell or utilize to establish “land-grant” colleges. These grants were provided to encourage the teaching of “practical agriculture, science, military science, and engineering” this was all done as a response to the industrial revolution2 , 3 . While the original Morril Act of 1862 provided educational incisions with swaths of land the later Morril Act of 1890 provided cash instead of land. I believe that we should follow a similar structure to the Morril Acts of 1890 and provide colleges with grant money to fund research focusing on agricultural research as it relates to data science and the internet of things. According to research done by the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council of Canada, it was found that the land grant college system was a major contributor to the faster growth rate of the US economy that led to its overtaking the United Kingdom as an economic superpower 4. Recreating a system during a quite similar time in history ( access to new technologies with vast unexplored potential ) would provide us with great economic benefits adding to the sustainability of Smart Farming according to Herket’s definition of Sustainability.

Finally, I propose that to ensure that the findings from this research are of benefit to all, the government ensures the findings from the research exist in the public domain and companies can take the research and create technologies based on them. The reason for this is to try and ward against the possibility of a monopoly being created in the smart farming sector. By placing these findings in the public domain we can ensure that larger companies with access to more money for R&D don’t have an extreme advantage and we can encourage competitiveness within the market.
